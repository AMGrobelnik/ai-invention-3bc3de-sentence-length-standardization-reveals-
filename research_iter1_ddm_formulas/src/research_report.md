## DDM Computation Methodology and Direct Standardization

### 1. Futrell et al. 2015: Core Formulas and Methods

Futrell et al. (2015) define mean dependency distance (MDD) for a sentence as the sum of absolute linear distances between each head-dependent pair, divided by the number of dependencies, where arc length equals the number of words between head and dependent including the dependent [1].

MDD_random is computed via Monte Carlo simulation, not an analytic formula: for each sentence, 100 random projective linearizations are generated by recursively ordering heads and dependents randomly [1]. Two baselines are used: a "free word order" baseline (fully random projective linearizations) and a "fixed word order" baseline (each relation type assigned a random weight in [-1,1], then ordered consistently) [1].

DLM strength is measured via a regression coefficient beta_3 that captures the extent to which dependency length of observed sentences grows more slowly with sentence length than random baselines, with significance tested via likelihood ratio tests (p < 0.0001) [1].

Within-treebank aggregation uses mixed-effects regression with sentence-level random intercepts and slopes, using squared sentence length (ls^2) as the primary predictor, restricted to sentences with length < 100 words [1]. This quadratic fit was better than linear for 33 of 37 languages [1].

Cross-treebank aggregation reports per-language significance via Stouffer's Z-transform test for sentences of length 12, with generalized additive model fits for visualization [1]. The study covers 37 languages from 10 language families using HamleDT 2.0, Google Universal Treebank 2.0, and Universal Dependencies 1.0 [1].

### 2. MDD_random Analytic Formula

The well-known analytic result is E[MDD_random(n)] = (n+1)/3 for a sentence of n words under uniform random (non-projective) word order [3, 4]. This serves as a theoretical upper baseline. Futrell et al.'s Monte Carlo projective baselines yield lower values than (n+1)/3 because projectivity constrains the space of permutations [1].

### 3. Petrini & Ferrer-i-Cancho 2022: Two-Regime Model

Petrini and Ferrer-i-Cancho propose a two-regime model for the distribution of dependency distances: the first regime follows exponential or power-law decay, and the second follows exponential decay [2]. The breakpoint averages 4-5 words across languages with low cross-linguistic variation, interpreted as reflecting a cognitive chunk-size constraint [2]. The model was tested on 20 languages and holds independently of sentence length and annotation style [2]. Two regimes are found in all languages when mixing sentence lengths, and robustly for most languages at fixed sentence lengths [2].

### 4. Yadav et al. 2022: Critique of DLM

Yadav, Mittal & Husain (2022) challenge DLM by proposing Intervener Complexity Minimization (ICM) — counting the number of syntactic heads intervening a dependency rather than raw word count [5]. When real trees are compared against IC-matched random arrangements controlling for tree topology, DLM no longer holds; they conclude DLM may arise as a consequence of ICM and arity restrictions [5]. Critically, they explicitly note that "an aggregate difference in dependency length between real trees and baseline trees could be subject to inaccuracies as the dependencies are mixed from different sentence lengths" [5] — directly identifying the sentence-length distribution confound.

### 5. Direct Standardization Formula

From epidemiology, the directly standardized rate is [6, 7]:

Rate_std = sum_i (rate_i * w_i), where w_i = N_ref(i) / N_ref(total)

Applied to DDM: DDM_std(L) = sum_n DDM_L(n) * P_ref(n), where DDM_L(n) is the mean dependency distance for language L at sentence length n, and P_ref(n) is the proportion of sentences of length n in a reference distribution [6, 7]. The reference population can be actual (e.g., pooled UD distribution) or synthetic; choice affects absolute values but not relative rankings [6]. Variance estimation would require bootstrap or delta method, as basic epidemiology texts do not provide closed-form variance for standardized rates [6].

### 6. Gap Confirmation

No prior work applies direct standardization (reweighting by sentence-length distribution) to DDM cross-linguistic rankings. Futrell et al. use regression controlling for sentence length but do not reweight distributions [1]. Yadav et al. note the confound but propose ICM rather than standardization [5]. This represents a genuine methodological gap.

### 7. Aggregation Methods Comparison

Liu (2008) uses simple averaging of sentence-level MDDs across a corpus [3]. Futrell et al. (2015) use mixed-effects regression with squared sentence length as predictor [1]. Temperley & Gildea (2018) use per-sentence comparison with random baselines [8]. None of these explicitly reweight by a standard sentence-length distribution [1, 3, 8].
